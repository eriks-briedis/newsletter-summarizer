Americans have been struggling for months with high gas prices and persistent inflation, sending consumer sentiment to a record low. Now retailers like Walmart and Lowes are getting louder about their warnings that spiking fuel costs driven by the US-Israel war with Iran will soon be reflected in the prices of products on their shelves.

When that happens, it’s only going to make US affordability problems that much worse. “We are concerned that the consumers have less ability to spend” even now, said Joe Feldman, an analyst at Telsey Advisory Group. Looking ahead, “the lower-income consumer is going to become even more challenged.”

At Walmart, Chief Financial Officer John David Rainey said the company has seen some habits change: consumers bought fewer gallons per visit at Walmart pumps in the first quarter, with the average number falling below 10 for the first time since 2022. “That’s an indication of stress,” he said.

Higher fuel costs haven’t yet pushed up prices of most goods in the US, according to the Trump administration. Affluent Americans have been propping up overall consumer spending, a primary engine for the economy; retail sales rose for a third-straight month in April, according to the US Department of Commerce.

But inflation concerns are still hitting consumer sentiment. The University of Michigan’s final May sentiment index decreased 5 points to 44.8 from April, according to the survey released Friday. That’s the latest in a series of record lows and weaker than all projections in a Bloomberg survey of economists.

—David E. Rovella

Tulsi Gabbard, citing her husband’s illness, became the latest Trump cabinet member to depart the administration. A former Democratic member of Congress who became a Republican, Gabbard was one of the most hotly contested of Donald Trump’s controversial cabinet nominations when she became Director of National Intelligence.

With no traditional intelligence service experience, she had previously expressed sympathy for Vladimir Putin and his war on Ukraine and was often praised by Russian state media. Democrats called Gabbard “compromised” during the confirmation process, warning against making her the most senior US intelligence official.

Gabbard had seemed increasingly out of step with the White House of late, as the US bombed Iran alongside Israel and earlier ordered an attack on Caracas and the renditioning of Venezuela President Nicolas Maduro. Gabbard had long expressed unease with US military intervention. Her adviser, Joe Kent, resigned in mid-March over the war in Iran, claiming in a public letter that Israel misled Trump into believing Iran posed an imminent threat.

An attack on a nuclear power plant in the United Arab Emirates has raised fears about the scope of Iran’s retaliation to any potential US or Israeli resumption of strikes, with experts highlighting the greater role Tehran-backed militias in Iraq are playing in the war.

Barakah is the Middle East’s largest nuclear power plant and one of two active in the region — Bushehr in southern Iran is the second. While there were no reported casualties or abnormal radiation levels, the attack underscored the vulnerability of civilian infrastructure to an escalation of hostilities in the region.

“This was a warning shot by Iran” about what might be in store for the UAE and the rest of the Gulf if hostilities with Israel and the US reignite, said Mohammed Baharoon, director of the Dubai Public Policy Research Center. “It’s also a way for Iran to keep pressure on Gulf states and tell them: ‘You won’t be immune and even if we don’t attack you directly, our proxies will.’”

Unsurprisingly, the UAE has made a more concerted push for an end to the war in recent days, joining Saudi Arabia and Qatar in urging Trump, who has spent the past few weeks issuing a series of threats and entreaties amid increasing pressure at home, to exit the conflict.

Anthropic is said to be near the close of its latest funding round, which may top $30 billion at a valuation above $900 billion, vaulting it ahead of rival OpenAI to become the world’s most valuable AI company.

Founded in 2021 by a group of former OpenAI employees, Anthropic has since emerged as a leader in the AI sector. The company has developed a series of artificial intelligence tools aimed at overhauling the way businesses handle tasks from coding to cybersecurity, and in doing so has shaken equities markets to their core. Anthropic and OpenAI are both expected to go public as soon as this fall, Bloomberg News has reported.

More than three weeks of anti-government blockades are choking off food, fuel and medicine supply in Bolivia’s administrative capital, La Paz, threatening to bring economic activity to a halt.

The national labor union, the La Paz farmers federation and supporters of former President Evo Morales are demanding the resignation of President Rodrigo Paz, a Trump ally who took office in November. The protesters say Paz has failed to fix the economy and is favoring elite business interests.

President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva is leading Brazil’s presidential election as Senator Flavio Bolsonaro, son of convicted former president Jair Bolsonaro, suffers from his ties to a man at the center of a massive banking scandal.

Lula leads Bolsonaro by 4 percentage points, 47% to 43%, in a hypothetical runoff election, according to a Datafolha survey published Friday. The poll is the latest to suggest Bolsonaro’s connection to jailed banker Daniel Vorcaro, former chief executive of Banco Master SA, is weighing on his candidacy ahead of the October election.
